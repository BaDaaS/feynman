## Segment 1 - Two outcomes (scene: `BlochPolesScene`, ~0:00-0:25)

The qubit is the smallest quantum system: its states live in two-dimensional
complex space. Take the observable sigma-z. It is Hermitian, so its eigenvalues are
real - that is the Part-0 theorem at work. And it squares to the identity, so those
eigenvalues square to one; they are plus one and minus one.

[cue: the plus-one state at the north pole, the minus-one state at the south pole]

A measurement of sigma-z has exactly two possible results. That is what makes this
system a bit - a quantum bit.

## Segment 2 - The bit flip (scene: `PauliFlipScene`, ~0:25-0:50)

The other Pauli operators do the same job along different axes. Sigma-x is the bit
flip: it sends the zero state to the one state and back again.

[cue: the up arrow flips to the down arrow]

Because sigma-x also squares to the identity, flipping twice returns you to where
you started. Each Pauli operator is Hermitian, traceless, and an involution: an
observable, a generator of rotations, and a two-outcome measurement, all at once.
These three algebraic facts, proven by direct computation, are the entire content
of "the qubit," and everything in quantum information is built on them.

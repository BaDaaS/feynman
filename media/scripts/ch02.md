## Segment 1 - The average of a measurement (scene: `ExpectationRealScene`, ~0:00-0:35)

A single measurement of an observable is random - you get one of its eigenvalues,
you cannot say which. What the theory predicts with certainty is the average over
many measurements, the expectation value. For a system in state psi, the
expectation of the observable A is the inner product of psi with A applied to psi.

For that number to be a physical prediction, it must be real. And it is, precisely
because A is self-adjoint.

[cue: as the state sweeps around the Bloch circle, the expectation value stays
pinned to the real axis, never leaving it]

Watch the expectation as we vary the state continuously. It slides along the real
line and never picks up an imaginary part. The reason is a two-line argument:
conjugating an inner product swaps its two slots, and self-adjointness lets you
move the operator from one slot to the other. Do both, and the expectation is equal
to its own conjugate - so it is real. This is the whole content of "an observable
predicts real averages," and it rests on nothing but self-adjointness.

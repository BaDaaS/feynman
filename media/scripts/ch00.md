## Segment 1 - Real spectra (scene: `RealSpectrumScene`, ~0:00-0:25)

Quantum mechanics begins with a modeling choice: a physical observable is a
self-adjoint operator. The word "self-adjoint" is doing real work. It forces the
possible measured values, which are the eigenvalues of the operator, to be real
numbers.

[cue: the two eigenvalues appear on the real axis]

And that is not an extra assumption; it follows from self-adjointness alone. If a
self-adjoint operator sends a nonzero vector to a scalar multiple of itself, that
scalar equals its own complex conjugate, and a number equal to its own conjugate
has no imaginary part. In Lean this is the theorem `selfAdjoint_eigenvalue_isReal`,
proved in two lines on top of Mathlib.

## Segment 2 - Diagonalization (scene: `SpectralDecompositionScene`, ~0:25-0:55)

Now restrict to finitely many dimensions, where the picture is complete and fully
verified. The spectral theorem says a Hermitian matrix is diagonalized by a
unitary change of basis. Concretely, the matrix factors as U, times a diagonal
matrix D of the real eigenvalues, times U-star.

[cue: the two eigenlines appear]

Geometrically: there are special directions, the eigenvectors, and along each of
them the operator does nothing but stretch.

[cue: the yellow vector along an eigenline is scaled, not rotated]

A generic vector gets rotated and stretched, but an eigenvector is only stretched,
by its eigenvalue. In the eigenbasis, the whole operator collapses to independent
scalings, one per axis. That is what the equation A equals U D U-star records, and
it is the theorem `hermitian_spectral_decomposition`.

## Segment 3 - Definite-value states (~0:55-1:15)

The same statement has a basis-free form: there is an orthonormal basis of
eigenvectors, and on the i-th one the operator acts by multiplying by the i-th
eigenvalue. That basis vector is the state in which the observable has a definite
value, namely that eigenvalue. This is `selfAdjoint_eigenvector_equation`.

These three facts, real spectrum, unitary diagonalization, and an orthonormal
eigenbasis, are the substrate the measurement rule will sit on. In the next chapter
we add the Born rule; later we recover this same shape in infinite dimensions,
where it becomes genuinely hard, and honest, work.

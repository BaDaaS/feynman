## Segment 1 - Which random? (scene: `BornDistributionScene`, ~0:00-0:35)

A measurement returns one eigenvalue, chosen at random. The Born rule tells you the
odds. Fix an orthonormal eigenbasis of the observable. The probability of the
outcome labeled i, when the system is in state psi, is the squared modulus of the
amplitude - the inner product of the i-th basis vector with psi, absolute value
squared.

[cue: two bars appear, the probabilities of outcomes zero and one for a qubit
state]

For this to be a probability rule, the numbers have to behave like probabilities.
They are nonnegative, because each is a squared length. And they sum to one.

[cue: the two bars, added, reach the p-equals-one line]

That last fact is not an extra assumption; it is Parseval's identity. The total
squared length of a vector is the sum of the squared lengths of its components in
an orthonormal basis. On a normalized state that total is one. So the Born rule is
automatically a probability distribution - and, as Gleason later showed, it is
essentially the only one the geometry allows.
